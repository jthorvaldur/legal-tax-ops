# Family Tax Coordination

How to coordinate tax obligations across family members, shared entities, and inter-generational transfers.

---

## Why Coordinate

Each family member files independently, but decisions by one member affect others:
- Income shifting between spouses (legal and illegal methods)
- Dependents claimed by one parent vs. the other
- Gift tax implications of transfers between family members
- Entity ownership shared across family
- Inheritance and estate planning
- FBAR/FATCA reporting for accounts family members have signature authority on

## Shared Professional Strategy

### Option A: Everyone Uses the Same CPA
- **Pros:** CPA sees the full picture, can optimize across family
- **Cons:** Conflict of interest if family members have disputes (e.g., divorce)
- **Best for:** Families that are aligned and cooperative

### Option B: Each Member Has Their Own CPA
- **Pros:** No conflicts, independent advice
- **Cons:** No coordination, potential missed optimization
- **Best for:** Families with potential disputes or complex individual situations

### Option C: Shared CPA for Coordination, Individual CPAs for Filing
- **Pros:** Best of both worlds. One professional sees the full picture and advises; individual CPAs execute.
- **Cons:** More expensive
- **Best for:** Families with significant cross-member tax considerations

## Common Family Tax Issues

### Dependents
- Only one parent can claim a child as a dependent per year
- In divorce: determined by custody agreement or IRS tie-breaker rules
- Form 8332: custodial parent can release claim to non-custodial parent
- Children's unearned income over $1,250 (2024) may be taxed at parent's rate ("kiddie tax")

### Gift Tax (US)
- Annual exclusion: $18,000 per recipient (2024) — no reporting required
- Gifts above annual exclusion require Form 709 but typically no tax (lifetime exemption ~$13.6M)
- Gifts between spouses: unlimited (if both US citizens)
- Gifts TO non-citizen spouse: $185,000 annual limit (2024)
- Gifts FROM foreign person to US person: report on Form 3520 if > $100K

### Family Loans
- Below-market loans between family members may trigger imputed interest (IRS AFR rates)
- Document all loans in writing (promissory note with terms, interest rate, repayment schedule)
- IRS scrutinizes family "loans" that look like gifts

### Children's Accounts (529, UTMA/UGMA)
- **529 Plans:** Tax-free growth for education expenses. Owner (usually parent) controls. Not counted as child's asset for most financial aid (FAFSA).
- **UTMA/UGMA:** Custodial accounts that become child's property at age of majority (18 or 21 depending on state). Irrevocable. Counted as child's asset.
- **Investment accounts in child's name:** Kiddie tax applies. First $1,250 untaxed, next $1,250 at child's rate, above at parent's rate.

## Year-End Family Tax Meeting

Before December 31 each year, sit down (or video call) as a family and cover:

1. **Entity check:** Any entities to create, dissolve, or restructure?
2. **Gift check:** Were any gifts > $18K made between family members?
3. **Dependent check:** Who is claiming which children this year?
4. **Income check:** Any major income events (sale of property, distributions, bonuses)?
5. **Estimated payment check:** Is everyone current on estimated taxes?
6. **Retirement contribution check:** Has everyone maximized contributions?
7. **Loss harvesting:** Any investment losses to realize before year-end?
8. **Professional check:** Does everyone have a tax professional engaged for the upcoming filing season?

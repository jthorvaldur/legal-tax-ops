# How to Evaluate and Work with Law Firms

What to look for, what to ask, and red flags — from the perspective of someone who has been through it.

---

## Before You Hire

### Questions to Ask in the First Call

1. **"What is your experience with [specific issue] in [specific court/jurisdiction]?"**
   - A good answer names judges, cites recent cases, describes outcomes
   - A bad answer is generic ("we handle all family law matters")

2. **"How do you bill, and what's the expected total cost to resolution?"**
   - Hourly is standard for litigation ($250-700/hr depending on market)
   - Get an estimate range. If they won't give one, that's a flag.
   - Ask: "What was the total cost of your last 3 similar cases?"

3. **"Who will actually work on my case?"**
   - Partners sell. Associates and paralegals do the work.
   - Know who your day-to-day contact will be

4. **"What is your communication policy?"**
   - How quickly do they respond to emails?
   - Do they bill for every email and phone call? (Most do)
   - Will they send you copies of everything filed?

5. **"What technology do you use?"**
   - If the answer is "Microsoft Word and email" — that tells you something
   - Better answers: Clio, Smokeball, Relativity, any practice management platform
   - This is a proxy for operational sophistication

### Red Flags

| Red Flag | What It Means |
|----------|--------------|
| Won't give a cost estimate | Either inexperienced or planning to bill open-ended |
| Discourages you from understanding the case | Wants you dependent, not informed |
| No written engagement letter | Unprofessional — walk away |
| Came from the same firm as opposing counsel | Potential conflict. Investigate. |
| Missed deadlines | If they miss one, they'll miss more |
| Bills for "research" on basic procedural questions | Padding hours |
| Won't return calls for days | You're not a priority |
| Aggressive promises ("we'll destroy them") | Litigation is uncertain. Promises are marketing. |

### Green Flags

| Green Flag | What It Means |
|------------|--------------|
| Explains your options with tradeoffs | Treats you as a decision-maker |
| Provides a realistic cost estimate | Has done this before |
| Uses practice management software | Operationally competent |
| Sends you copies of everything | Transparency |
| Responds within 24 hours | You matter |
| Explains what the judge is likely to do | Knows the judge and the court |
| Suggests settlement paths | Understands that winning isn't always litigating |

## How Good Firms Actually Operate

### Document Management
- Every document tagged by date, type, party, and relevance
- Chronological index maintained throughout the case
- Every factual claim linked to a source document
- Deposition testimony cross-referenced against prior statements

### Case Strategy
- Written case theory document (updated as facts develop)
- Motion strategy mapped with contingencies
- Judge research (ruling patterns, preferences, pet peeves)
- Opposing counsel research (litigation style, prior cases, tendencies)

### Communication
- Client updates at defined intervals (weekly or biweekly)
- Pre-hearing prep calls with the client
- Post-hearing debrief with next steps
- Everything in writing (email trail)

### What You Can Replicate Without a Firm
- Maintain your own chronological document index
- Write your own case theory (1-2 pages: what happened, what you want, why)
- Research your judge on Trellis or through public records
- Research opposing counsel's prior cases
- Prep yourself before every hearing with written notes
- Keep a contemporaneous log of everything that happens

## Working With Your Lawyer Effectively

### Do
- Send organized information (not raw dumps)
- Respond to requests quickly
- Keep your own copy of every document
- Ask questions when you don't understand
- Be honest about facts, even unfavorable ones
- Request itemized billing statements monthly

### Don't
- Call for emotional support at $400/hr — that's what therapists are for
- Withhold unfavorable facts — your lawyer needs the full picture
- Ignore their advice then blame them for the outcome
- Send 15 emails about the same issue — consolidate
- Expect them to remember every detail — send written summaries

## When to Fire Your Lawyer

1. **They've missed a deadline** that has consequences
2. **They're not communicating** despite repeated requests
3. **They're billing for work not done** (ask for detailed time entries)
4. **They don't understand your case** after months of representation
5. **They're making strategic decisions without consulting you**
6. **You've lost trust** — this relationship is fiduciary. Trust is foundational.

### How to Fire Them
- Send a written notice (email + letter)
- Request your complete file (they must provide it)
- File a substitution of counsel or withdrawal with the court
- Don't leave a gap — have new counsel or be prepared to go pro se
- Settle any outstanding bills to avoid liens on your case

## Solo / Boutique vs. Large Firm

| Factor | Solo/Boutique | Large Firm |
|--------|--------------|------------|
| Cost | $200-400/hr | $400-1,000+/hr |
| Attention | You're 1 of 20-50 clients | You're 1 of 200+ |
| Specialization | Often deeper in one area | Broader resources |
| Technology | Varies widely | Usually better tools |
| Responsiveness | Usually faster | Often slower |
| Trial experience | Varies — ask | More likely to have litigators |
| Best for | Straightforward cases, family law, small business | Complex commercial, multi-jurisdiction, bet-the-company |

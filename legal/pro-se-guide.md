# Operating as a Self-Represented Party (Pro Se)

A practical guide to representing yourself in court — what works, what to avoid, and how to compensate for not having a law firm behind you.

---

## The Reality

Courts are required to give pro se litigants some leeway on procedural matters, but they won't do your job for you. The judge expects you to:
- Meet deadlines
- Follow formatting rules
- Present evidence properly
- Make coherent legal arguments
- Behave professionally

You won't get a pass on substance, but you may get patience on procedure.

---

## Before You Go Pro Se

Ask yourself:
1. **Can I actually do this?** Complex cases with significant stakes (custody, criminal, high-value property) often need a lawyer.
2. **Why am I pro se?** If it's cost, explore limited-scope representation (lawyer handles parts, you handle the rest).
3. **What's the worst case?** If losing means jail, losing your children, or financial ruin, strongly consider finding counsel.

---

## The Pro Se Advantage

You actually have some advantages over represented parties:

- **You know your case better than any lawyer would** — no one has more incentive to get the facts right
- **You don't bill by the hour** — you can invest unlimited time in preparation
- **Judges often perceive pro se parties sympathetically** — especially against well-funded opposing counsel
- **You can be direct** — no lawyer-to-lawyer filtering of information
- **You can move faster** — no scheduling around your lawyer's other cases

## Critical Rules

### 1. Never miss a deadline
This is the #1 way pro se litigants lose. Calendar every deadline. Set reminders 30 days, 14 days, and 3 days before.

### 2. File everything in writing
Oral arguments are forgotten. Written filings are on the record forever. If something matters, put it in a motion or response.

### 3. Be professional, not emotional
The judge evaluates credibility. Emotional outbursts, personal attacks, or sarcasm destroy credibility. Be respectful, factual, and organized.

### 4. Know your local rules
Every court has local rules on formatting, filing, page limits, and procedures. Get them from the court's website. Follow them exactly.

### 5. Serve everything on opposing counsel
If you file it with the court, you must serve it on the other side. Certificate of Service on every filing.

## Document Preparation Standards

### Caption
Every document needs the proper caption:
- Court name
- Case number
- Parties
- Document title

Check your jurisdiction's format — some require specific spacing, bold, or underlines.

### Structure
Professional motions follow this pattern:
1. **Caption**
2. **Introduction** (1-2 sentences: who you are and what you want)
3. **Background** (relevant facts)
4. **Argument** (why the law supports your request)
5. **Prayer for Relief** (exactly what you're asking the court to do)
6. **Signature block** (name, address, contact info)
7. **Certificate of Service**

### Citations
When citing law:
- Statutes: full citation (e.g., 750 ILCS 5/510(a))
- Cases: full citation first time (e.g., *In re Marriage of Sharp*, 369 Ill. App. 3d 271, 280 (2d Dist. 2006)), then short form (*Sharp*, 369 Ill. App. 3d at 280)
- Always verify citations are real — see `ai-for-law.md` for warnings about AI-generated fake cases

## Courtroom Conduct

### Appearance
- Dress professionally (suit or business attire)
- Arrive 15-30 minutes early
- Bring organized copies of everything you've filed
- Bring a notepad and pen

### Speaking
- Stand when addressing the judge
- "Your Honor" — always
- Never interrupt the judge or opposing counsel
- Keep statements brief and factual
- If you don't understand a question, ask for clarification

### What to Bring
- [ ] Copy of all filed documents (organized by document number)
- [ ] Key exhibits in separate folders
- [ ] Written outline of your argument (2-3 key points max)
- [ ] Notepad for taking notes
- [ ] Calendar (for scheduling)
- [ ] Case number written down (you will be asked)

## Building Your Case

### The Case Binder
Organize a physical or digital binder:
1. **Tab 1: Case summary** — 1-page overview of what the case is about
2. **Tab 2: Timeline** — chronological list of every event
3. **Tab 3: Filed documents** — everything you've filed, in order
4. **Tab 4: Opposing party's filings** — everything they've filed
5. **Tab 5: Court orders** — every order entered
6. **Tab 6: Exhibits** — evidence you plan to present
7. **Tab 7: Law** — statutes and cases you're relying on
8. **Tab 8: Correspondence** — emails with opposing counsel

### Evidence Rules Basics
- **Hearsay:** Out-of-court statements offered to prove the truth of the matter. Generally inadmissible, but many exceptions.
- **Authentication:** You must prove a document is what you claim it is. Bank records need a custodian or self-authentication.
- **Relevance:** Evidence must relate to an issue in the case.
- **Foundation:** Before presenting evidence, you must lay a foundation (explain what it is, where it came from, why it's reliable).

## Getting Help Without Hiring a Lawyer

### Limited Scope Representation (Unbundled Legal Services)
A lawyer handles specific parts of your case:
- Review your filings before you submit them
- Appear at one hearing
- Draft one motion
- Provide strategy advice

**Cost:** $200-500 per task, much less than full representation

### Legal Aid
- Contact your local legal aid society
- Many have pro bono programs for family law
- Income limits apply

### Law School Clinics
- Many law schools run clinics where supervised students provide free help
- Quality varies but often excellent (professors are supervising)

### Self-Help Centers
- Many courthouses have self-help centers for pro se litigants
- Free assistance with forms and procedures
- Cannot give legal advice (but can explain how things work)

### Paralegal Services
- Paralegals can prepare documents under your direction
- Cannot give legal advice or represent you in court
- $50-150/hour (much less than lawyers)
- Good for: document formatting, filing, service, deadline tracking
